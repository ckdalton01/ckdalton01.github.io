Endpoint Compliance Policies: Avoiding ‘Not Compliant’ Status with Intune

The Problem

Endpoint compliance policies are vital for enforcing security standards on devices managed by Intune. However, encountering devices persistently stuck in a ‘Not Compliant’ state is a frustratingly common experience. This often stems from misconfigured policies, conflicting settings, or unexpected behavior related to device health attestation, device constraints, enrollment profiles, and even conditional access. This guide will provide a detailed breakdown of common issues and how to resolve them to maintain compliance and a positive user experience.

Implementation/Steps

1. Understanding Device Health Attestation (DHA)

Device Health Attestation is a crucial component. It’s the device’s self-assessment of its health status (OS version, anti-malware, etc.). Failing DHA is a primary cause of non-compliance.

  • Troubleshooting DHA Failures:
    • Check the Intune Portal: Navigate to Devices > All Devices. Select the device and review the ‘Device Health Attestation’ section. Look for errors and timestamps.
    • Event Logs: On the device itself (if possible), review the Event Viewer (Application and System logs) for errors related to Intune Management Extension.
    • Intune Management Extension: Ensure the Intune Management Extension is installed and up-to-date on the device. Outdated versions can cause attestation issues.
    • Firewall/Proxy: Verify that the device can communicate with the required Microsoft endpoints for attestation.
    • Hybrid Azure AD Join: For Hybrid Azure AD joined devices, synchronization issues can impact DHA. Ensure proper synchronization.
  • Policy Configuration: Sometimes, the policy itself triggers DHA. Review the Device Health Attestation policy in Intune and confirm no accidental restrictions are being applied.

2. Configuring Device Constraints Effectively

Device constraints enforce specific requirements (e.g., encryption, password complexity). Overly restrictive constraints are a frequent cause of non-compliance.

  • Encryption: Ensure the encryption method specified in the policy matches the device’s capabilities. BitLocker requires hardware-supported encryption.
  • Password Complexity: Carefully consider the complexity requirements. Excessive restrictions can prevent users from setting passwords and lead to frustration. Balance security with usability.
  • OS Version: Enforcing a specific OS version can quickly put older devices out of compliance. Consider allowing a range of supported versions.
  • Security Policy: Verify that the security policy enforced by the compliance policy doesn’t conflict with existing group policies or local security settings.
  • Example PowerShell to Check Constraint Status (on a single device):
# Requires the Microsoft Graph PowerShell module
Connect-MgGraph -Scopes "DeviceManagementManagedDevices.Read.All"

$DeviceID = "YOUR_DEVICE_ID" # Replace with the actual device ID

$Device = Get-MgDeviceManagementManagedDevice -ManagedDeviceId $DeviceID

Write-Host "Device Name: $($Device.DeviceName)"

foreach ($Constraint in $Device.ComplianceState.CompliantDevicePolicies.PolicyComplianceList) {
    Write-Host "  Policy: $($Constraint.PolicyName)"
    Write-Host "    Status: $($Constraint.ComplianceState)"
}

3. Conditional Access Considerations

Conditional Access policies complement compliance policies. If a device isn’t compliant, Conditional Access can block access to corporate resources.

  • Review Conditional Access Policies: Ensure that your Conditional Access policies are correctly configured and don’t inadvertently block compliant devices.
  • Device Compliance Reporting: Utilize the Intune portal’s reporting capabilities to monitor device compliance status and identify trends.
  • Gradual Rollout: When implementing or changing Conditional Access policies, perform a phased rollout to minimize disruption.

4. Device Enrollment Profiles and Impact

Device enrollment profiles define how devices join Intune. Incorrect profiles can hinder compliance.

  • Verify Enrollment Method: Ensure the enrollment method (e.g., User-initiated enrollment, Automatic enrollment) aligns with your organization’s requirements.
  • Profile Conflicts: Conflicting enrollment profiles can cause issues. Review and consolidate profiles as necessary.
  • Device Enrollment Manager (DEM): For iOS/macOS devices enrolled through DEM, verify that the DEM agent is properly installed and configured.

Best Practices/Pro-Tip

Real-World Tip: Before deploying a new or updated compliance policy, create a test group of devices representing different OS versions and hardware configurations. Thoroughly test the policy in this group before rolling it out to your entire organization. This prevents widespread non-compliance and allows for adjustments based on real-world feedback.

Verification

  • Intune Portal: Regularly monitor the Intune portal’s Devices > All Devices view to verify device compliance status.
  • PowerShell: Use the Get-MgDeviceManagementManagedDevice cmdlet (as shown in the example above) to check the compliance status of individual devices.
  • User Feedback: Actively solicit feedback from users regarding any issues they experience with device compliance.
